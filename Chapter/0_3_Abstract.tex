Capacitive sensing technology has become a standard for touch-based interaction, yet most systems treat all finger contacts as uniform inputs. Recogning and classifying specific finger types (e.g., thumb, index, or middle finger) from capacitive images can significantly enhance human-computer interaction and device ergonomics. However, deploying such recognition models on battery-constrained edge devices requires a careful balance between classification accuracy and power efficiency.

This thesis investigates the development of efficient deep learning models for capacitive image-based finger recognition, with a particular focus on Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs)—a bio-inspired, event-driven computing paradigm known for its low power consumption. A baseline Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) is first established to provide a performance reference. Building upon this, an equivalent SNN architecture is designed to enable direct comparison in terms of recognition capability and computational efficiency. Furthermore, the study extends to transformer-based architectures by incorporating global attention mechanisms within a Spiking Vision Transformer (Spiking ViT) framework.

The proposed models are systematically evaluated in terms of classification accuracy across multiple finger categories, as well as their computational and energy efficiency. \textcolor{red}{[Insert your results here: e.g., The Spiking ViT achieves an accuracy of X\% while reducing energy consumption by Y\% compared to the CNN baseline]}. The findings highlight the potential of SNN-based architectures as a competitive and energy-efficient alternative to conventional deep learning approaches for finger-type recognition in next-generation interactive systems.
